## **Theory of Change**

AdaRSS is built on the premise that global workforce instability is driven by a fundamental information failure: individuals, institutions, and governments lack reliable, structured intelligence about the long-term durability of skills in a rapidly evolving economy. As a result, decisions around education, hiring, and workforce investment are made using short-term signals such as current job demand or historical curricula, rather than predictive understanding of skill longevity.

This leads to misaligned investments in education and training, widespread skill obsolescence, inefficient labor allocation, and reduced economic mobility. These effects compound over time, particularly in economies where adaptive capacity and access to real-time labor intelligence are limited.

AdaRSS intervenes by introducing a structured intelligence system that classifies skills based on long-term economic stability and market evolution patterns. Through machine learning models trained on skill representations and labor dynamics, the system categorizes skills into enduring, emergent, and transient classes, transforming unstructured workforce signals into actionable intelligence.

This classification layer enables a set of interventions across the ecosystem. Individuals gain clearer guidance on career direction and skill investment decisions. Educational institutions can align curricula with long-term workforce demand rather than historical course structures. Workforce development agencies can design more effective reskilling programs based on predictive skill trajectories. Employers and governments gain a structured foundation for talent planning, labor forecasting, and policy design.

As adoption scales, these localized improvements aggregate into system-level effects: reduced skill misallocation, improved alignment between education and labor markets, increased workforce adaptability, and more efficient allocation of human capital across industries and regions. Over time, this contributes to higher productivity, reduced unemployment friction, and improved economic resilience.

In the long-term scenario, AdaRSS functions as a foundational layer of workforce intelligence infrastructure, enabling a shift from reactive, assumption-based workforce systems to predictive, data-driven human capital planning. This transition supports not only individual career stability, but also broader economic stability in the face of accelerating technological change.
